2. Comprehensive Findings (original + gap research merged)
A. Core consensus (Tabs 1, 2, 4, 5, 6)
- Queen is more powerful by practical fighting criteria:
  - Movement: can move any number of squares horizontally, vertically, or diagonally (rook + bishop combined).
  - Effects: controls large areas of the board and enables strong tactical threats (e.g., forks, line control).
  - Heuristic value: typically ~9 points.
- King is more important/decisive by objective/value criteria:
  - Movement: one square in any direction; cannot move into check.
  - Decisive condition: the game ends when the King is checkmated.
  - Value framing: “infinite/priceless” or “no point value” in the normal trade sense because it cannot be traded without losing.

B. Metric framing: “power” vs “importance” (Tabs 1, 2, 4, 5, 6)
- Multiple panels explicitly or implicitly resolve the comparison by separating:
  - “Power” = mobility/attack/board control (Queen dominates).
  - “Importance/value” = win-condition centrality (King dominates).

C. Phase-based nuance (Tabs 2, 4, 5, 6)
- The King’s practical role shifts by phase:
  - Opening/midgame: often treated as a liability that must be protected.
  - Endgame: becomes more active as a fighting piece when major threats are reduced.
- Numeric “minor-piece-like” endgame framing:
  - Tab6: king’s endgame fighting capability “around 3 or 4 points.”
  - Tab5: endgame activity “akin to a minor piece (valued ~3 points).”
  - Tabs 2 and 4 support increased endgame activity but do not attach a number.

D. Sacrifice and risk framing (Tabs 1, 2, 5)
- Queen sacrifices:
  - Tab1 and Tab5 note queens can be sacrificed to achieve checkmate; Tab2 notes sacrifice is possible if it leads to a win.
- Queen downside / risk:
  - Tab2: queen’s high value makes it a constant target; losing it for less is often game-losing.
  - Tab5 expands: queens can overextend; can be vulnerable to being trapped; may lose relative edge in closed positions (while still generally versatile overall).
